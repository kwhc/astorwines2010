Astor's Guide to Saké



Sakés can be divided into two groups – those made only from water, rice, and koji (a type of mold that acts as a fermenting agent), and those to which a small amount of brewer’s alcohol is added after the fermentation. The former is called Junmai, or “pure rice saké,” and the fortified type is called Honjozo. If the label does not explicitly state that a saké is Junmai, then it can be assumed to be Honjozo.

For straight Junmai or Honjozo sakés, the grains of rice have been polished down to 70% or less of their original mass. The next level of polishing is Ginjo, for which the grains are polished down to 60% or less. The highest degree of polishing is termed Daiginjo, and at this level the grains of rice have been polished down to a meager 50% or less of their original mass.

To confuse the matter slightly: The polishing grade has nothing to do with whether or not any brewer’s alcohol has been added to the saké. Thus, for pure sakés to which no alcohol has been added, “Junmai” is used as a prefix: e.g. Junmai Ginjo and Junmai Daiginjo. If alcohol has been added, the sakés will simply be labeled as Ginjo or Daiginjo.

While there is indeed a direct correlation between the level of polishing and the relative price of a saké, that’s not to say that Junmai Daiginjo sakés are necessarily “better” than simple Junmai sakés. Nonetheless, the more highly polished the grains of rice are, the cleaner and more refined the resultant flavors and aromas will be. So, if you prefer sakés that are light, delicate, and fruity, then you may indeed want to shell out for some of the more expensive Ginjo and Daiginjo styles. If, however, you prefer sakés that are more robust and earthy, you may find that the Junmai and Honjozo styles suit your palate better.

An important note: If you want to serve your saké warm, stick to the Junmai and Honjozo styles. Ginjo and Daiginjo sakés are too delicate to withstand the heat.

The classification of sakés doesn’t end with their polishing grade: there are several further discriminating terms that can be applied to any saké, at any level of polishing. Here’s a quick breakdown of the most common lingo:

Genshu sakés are undiluted – i.e., “cask strength” or “barrel strength.” (And yes, to answer your raising eyebrows, most sakés are indeed diluted with water prior to bottling.) As you might imagine, Genshu sakés tend to offer much sharper and more pronounced flavors. 

Koshu (or Jukushu) sakés are aged. These sakés generally take on a golden amber hue, and often develop complex aromas of nuts and smoke.

Nama sakés are unpasteurized and are often released seasonally. These tend to be particularly delicate and must be kept refrigerated at all times. They generally offer very subtle fruity aromas.

Nigori sakés are the cloudy-looking ones on the shelf: they do not undergo the traditional filtration process. These sakés range from dry to sweet, and generally possess a creamy texture and lots of fruit on the palate. Nigori sakés should be gently tipped upside down in the bottle a few times prior to serving.

Shizuku is “trickle” or “free-run” saké. It tends to be extremely delicate and, as one might expect, quite pricey.

Tokubetsu means “special,” and indicates the use of more highly polished rice than would typically be denoted by the polishing grade, or the use of “very special” saké rice.
